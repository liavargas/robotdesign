Introduction:

Research has shown that humans develop perceieved notions of the social and functional capabilities of a robot simply by looking at it. For example, an observer will make assumptions if a robot is a male or female based on its waist-to-hip ratio. These perceived notions influence an individual's expectations of how the robot should behave and function [1].

  • When expectations for how the robot should act do not agree with the true capabilies of the robot → user dissatisfaction sentiment_dissatisfied
  • When they agree → facilitates positive human-robot interaction (HRI) sentiment_satisfied_alt [2]
Therefore, it is important to learn how to design a robot that corresponds with its purpose and how it will act socially.


Background

A research paper consisting of 3 studies was conducted with the goal of using design metaphors to understand user's perceptions of socially interactive robots (SIR). The study used a dataset of 165 SIR embodiments to asses a user's social and functional expectations for the robot. Users participated in each of the three studies via Mechanical Turk survey questions.

  • A modified version of the Robotic Social Attributes Scale (RoSAS) [3] was used to measure warmth, competence and discomfort
  • Masculinity and Femininity were measured using the Bem Sex-Role Inventory Scale [4]
  • Social Role [5], Identity Closeness [6] and Likeability from the Godspeed scale [7] were all measured on a 9 point differential scale further explained in the attribute definitions
  • A modified version of the Embodiment and Corporeality of Artificial Agents Scale (EmCorp-Scale) [8] was used to measure user perceptions of the functional attributes: shared perceptions & interpretation, tactile interaction & mobility and non-verbal expressiveness

Social Perception

Functional Perception

  • Warmth: related to the perception that another agent may want to help or harm us
  • Competence: related to the perception that another agent has the ability to help or harm us
  • Discomfort: related to the awkwardness of the robot
  • Masculinity: how masculine the robot appears
  • Femininity: how feminine the robot appears
  • Social Role: measure of the interaction dynamics between the person and robot in an interaction: 1 = subordinate, 5 = robot is a peer, 9 = robot is a superior
  • Likeability: 1 indicates raters strongly "disliked" the robot and 9 indicates the rater “strongly likes” the robot
  • Identity Closeness: measures the degree of in-group identification of the person with the robot. 1 = robot looks nothing like the rater, 9 = robot is exactly like the rater
  • Shared perception & Interpretation: is a measure of the perceived perceptual capabilities (such as vision and hearing of the robot)
  • Tactile Interaction & Mobility: a measure of the perceived ability of the robot to navigate in space and physically contact humans and objects
  • Non-verbal Expressiveness: Relates to the robot’s ability to use gestures and facial expressions
